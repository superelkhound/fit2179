What Does Your Degree Actually Get You?

Graduate employment outcomes across Australian fields of study — QILT 2024 & ABS 2025

Choosing a degree is one of the biggest decisions you'll make — and one of the most expensive. Tuition, lost income, years of your life. Most people make that decision based on interest, reputation, or gut feel. This visualisation uses the latest Australian graduate data to show what different degrees actually deliver: who finds work, how quickly, how much they earn, and where the gaps are. The data won't make the decision for you — but it might change what questions you ask.

Which degrees lead to full-time work — and what do they pay?

One year after graduating, the difference between fields is stark. Rehabilitation, Pharmacy and Medicine graduates find full-time work at rates above 90% — while fewer than half of Creative Arts graduates are in full-time employment. Use the dropdown to filter by discipline group, then click any bar to see where that field sits in the bubble plot on the right.

The bubble plot shows three things at once: how likely graduates are to find full-time work (left–right), how much they typically earn (up–down), and how many employed graduates are working part-time (bubble size). Note that bubble size and left–right position measure different things — a field can have a strong full-time employment rate while also having a large bubble, because many of those who are employed chose part-time. Nursing is a good example: strong employment rate, but a large bubble because shift work and part-time arrangements are common in the sector. Hold Shift to select multiple fields. Double-click to deselect.

How do fields stack up across every measure at once?

Each cell shows how a field performs relative to all others on four key outcomes — employment rate, median salary, part-time rate, and gender pay gap. Darker blue means better performance. Part-time rate is shown inverted, so darker still means fewer graduates working part-time. Fields like Medicine and Rehabilitation sit dark across most columns. Creative Arts and Communications sit consistently light. Hover over any cell for the exact figures.

Part-time work: choice or compromise?

Not all part-time work means things went wrong. This chart splits part-time employment into two groups: graduates who are satisfied with their hours (blue) and those actively seeking more work (red dots). Creative Arts and Psychology graduates have the highest part-time rates overall — but a significant share of that is involuntary. In contrast, fields like Nursing have moderate part-time rates where most graduates appear to have chosen those arrangements.

Is the graduate job market getting better or worse?

Looking at the past three years, the picture is mixed. Full-time employment rates peaked in 2023 and dipped in 2024 — suggesting the post-pandemic jobs surge may be easing. But median salaries have risen steadily every year, meaning graduates who do find full-time work are earning more than their predecessors. Whether that trade-off continues is worth watching.

Which fields saw the biggest salary jump in the last year?

Each line connects a field's median salary in 2023 (left) to 2024 (right). Steeper lines mean bigger change. Architecture and Dentistry stand out in blue — both saw gains of more than $8,000 in a single year. Tourism and Hospitality is the only field where median salary fell. Most fields cluster in a narrow band of modest growth. Hover over any line to see exact figures.

The gender pay gap looks different depending on what you study

Across almost every field, male graduates out-earn female graduates one year after finishing their degree. Tourism and Hospitality and Architecture show the largest gaps. Communications is the notable exception — one of the few fields where female graduates report higher median salaries than their male counterparts. These are median figures for full-time workers, so the gap reflects starting salaries rather than lifetime earnings.

Where you live affects how qualified your neighbours are — but maybe not you

The ACT stands out clearly — Canberra's concentration of public service, research and policy roles means a higher share of working-age residents hold a bachelor degree or above than anywhere else in Australia. Tasmania, Queensland and Western Australia trail the national average. This reflects the existing workforce rather than graduate outcomes directly, but it gives a sense of where degree-holders tend to end up working — and where competition for graduate roles may be lower.

Does it matter how far you go with your education?

Broadly, yes — but perhaps less than you'd expect once you're past Year 12. Postgraduate holders have the highest employment rates, but the gap between a bachelor degree and a certificate narrows considerably. The real cliff is below Year 12: employment rates drop sharply for those who didn't complete secondary school. For most people reading this, the question isn't whether to study — it's what and how far.

When graduates end up in the wrong job, why?

Among graduates working in roles below their qualification level, the single biggest reason is "entry level job / career stepping stone" — suggesting that for many, underemployment is a temporary phase rather than a dead end. Lack of suitable local jobs is the most common structural barrier. Together, these two reasons account for nearly a third of all responses — pointing to both patience and geography as factors graduates can actually act on.

No dataset tells the full story. These figures reflect outcomes one year after graduation — careers look different at five years, ten years, or after a postgraduate qualification. They also reflect medians: half of graduates in every field earn more than the figure shown, and half earn less. What the data does show clearly is that field of study matters more than most people assume — and that the gap between the best and worst outcomes is wide enough to be worth understanding before you enrol.

Data sources: Graduate Outcomes Survey 2024, Quality Indicators for Learning and Teaching (QILT), Australian Government Department of Education. Available at qilt.edu.au. Undergraduate completers surveyed approximately one year after graduation.

ABS Education and Work, Australia, May 2025, Australian Bureau of Statistics. Available at abs.gov.au.

Both datasets are publicly available under open licence. Data pre-aggregated from source files; no values have been modified or fabricated.
